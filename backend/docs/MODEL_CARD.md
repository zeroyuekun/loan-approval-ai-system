## Intended Use

- **Primary use**: Automated loan approval/denial decisions for Australian consumer credit applications.
- **Secondary uses**: Risk grading (AAA through CCC), pricing support, portfolio management, expected loss estimation (PD x LGD x EAD).
- **Out of scope**: Not intended for non-Australian lending markets, commercial or institutional lending exceeding $5M, or behavioural scoring over the life of a loan.

## Model Architecture

### Primary Model

- **Algorithm**: XGBoost (`XGBClassifier`)
- **Hyperparameter search**: Optuna Bayesian optimization (TPE sampler) with 50 trials, 3-fold cross-validation, scoring on AUC-ROC
- **Search space**: n_estimators [200-600], max_depth [4-10], learning_rate [0.01-0.15 log], subsample [0.6-1.0], min_child_weight [1-10], colsample_bytree [0.6-1.0], reg_lambda [1-50 log], reg_alpha [0.01-10 log], gamma [0-5]
- **Early stopping**: 30 rounds on validation set (refitted after search)
- **Class imbalance**: `scale_pos_weight` = (count of denied) / (count of approved)

### Probability Calibration

Isotonic regression fitted on the held-out validation set (10% of data). The `_CalibratedModel` wrapper passes raw XGBoost probabilities through the isotonic calibrator and clips to [0, 1].

### Monotonic Constraints

33 features have enforced monotonic constraints during XGBoost tree construction:

- **16 positive** (higher value improves approval): annual_income, credit_score, employment_length, has_cosigner, income_credit_interaction, serviceability_ratio, employment_stability, deposit_ratio, net_monthly_surplus, income_per_dependant, credit_score_x_tenure, credit_history_months, savings_balance, salary_credit_regularity, avg_monthly_savings_rate, document_consistency_score.
- **17 negative** (higher value worsens approval): debt_to_income, has_bankruptcy, loan_to_income, expense_to_income, credit_card_burden, lvr_x_dti, monthly_repayment_ratio, num_credit_enquiries_6m, worst_arrears_months, num_defaults_5yr, num_bnpl_accounts, num_dishonours_12m, days_in_overdraft_12m, income_verification_gap, enquiry_intensity, bureau_risk_score, rate_stress_buffer.
- **8 unconstrained**: has_hecs, lvr, total_open_accounts, is_existing_customer, rba_cash_rate, unemployment_rate, property_growth_12m, consumer_confidence.

`lvr` is intentionally unconstrained because non-home loans have LVR = 0.0, which is semantically "no property collateral" rather than "best possible LVR". `has_hecs` is unconstrained because its effect is income-mediated and not uniformly negative.

### Conformal Prediction

Split conformal method using nonconformity scores (|predicted_prob - actual_outcome|) computed on the validation set. At inference time, the 95th percentile quantile of these scores defines the prediction interval width, providing guaranteed coverage.

### Reject Inference

Parcelling method: denied applications from the training split are augmented back into the training set at 0.5x sample weight, mitigating selection bias from training only on approved loans. When combined with fairness reweighting, the two weight vectors are multiplied and renormalised.

### Fairness Reweighting

Sample weights equalise representation across employment type groups during training. Weight per group = (total / n_groups) / group_count. This reduces disparate impact across employment types (permanent, casual, self-employed, contract).

### Outcome Tracking (SR 11-7 Compliance)

SR 11-7 (Federal Reserve, 2011) identifies outcomes analysis as one of three model
validation pillars: comparing model predictions to actual results.

This system tracks:
- Binary outcome comparison (predicted approval/denial vs. actual performing/default)
- Accuracy by risk grade (AAA through CCC)
- Calibration gap (predicted vs. actual default rates)
- Vintage analysis (default rates by origination month)

Actual outcomes are recorded on `LoanApplication.actual_outcome` with categories:
performing, arrears_30, arrears_60, arrears_90, default, prepaid.

### Threshold Selection

Three threshold strategies are computed during training:

1. **F1-optimal**: maximises F1 score across the threshold sweep (0.05-0.95).
2. **Youden's J**: maximises TPR - FPR (statistical optimum).
3. **Cost-optimal**: minimises banking cost function where FP cost = 5x FN cost (a false positive — approving a defaulter — is far more expensive than a false negative). This reflects APRA APS 220 credit risk weighting.

The operational threshold is stored in `ModelVersion.optimal_threshold` and defaults to 0.60.

## Fairness Evaluation

### Protected Attributes Monitored

- `employment_type` (payg_permanent, payg_casual, self_employed, contract)
- `applicant_type` (single, couple)
- `state` (NSW, VIC, QLD, WA, SA, TAS, ACT, NT)

### Metrics

- **Disparate Impact Ratio** (EEOC 80% rule): min(group approval rate) / max(group approval rate). Must be >= 0.80.
- **Equalized Odds Difference**: max gap in TPR or FPR across groups within each protected attribute.
- **Per-group breakdown**: count, actual approval rate, predicted approval rate, TPR, and FPR for each group.

### Mitigation

Fairness reweighting applied during training (see Model Architecture section). Without reweighting, employment type disparate impact was approximately 0.38, well below the 0.80 threshold.

### Fairness: Impossibility Theorem and Design Choices

Kleinberg, Mullainathan, and Raghavan (2016) proved that three common fairness
criteria — calibration, false positive rate balance, and false negative rate balance
— **cannot be simultaneously satisfied** except under perfect prediction or equal
base rates across groups. Chouldechova (2017) independently proved the same result.

Since default rates differ across demographic groups in practice, this system
**prioritises calibration** (predictive parity) as the primary fairness criterion,
because accurate risk pricing is essential for responsible lending under the NCCP Act.

The 80% disparate impact threshold (four-fifths rule) is used as a regulatory
screening tool. Per the EEOC Uniform Guidelines (29 CFR §1607.4), this threshold
is explicitly "not intended as a legal definition" but "a practical means of keeping
the attention of the enforcement agencies on serious discrepancies."

**Intersectional fairness testing** is performed across pairwise combinations of
protected attributes (employment_type × applicant_type, etc.) following Kim et al.
(2023), who demonstrated that single-axis compliance can mask amplified disparities
at intersections.

**References:**
- Kleinberg, J., Mullainathan, S., & Raghavan, M. (2016). "Inherent Trade-Offs in
  the Fair Determination of Risk Scores." arXiv:1609.05807.
- Chouldechova, A. (2017). "Fair prediction with disparate impact." *Big Data*, 5(2).
  arXiv:1703.00056.
- Kim et al. (2023). "Fair Models in Credit: Intersectional Discrimination and the
  Amplification of Inequity." arXiv:2308.02680.
- EEOC Uniform Guidelines, 29 CFR §1607.4.

## Explainability

### SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations)

`shap.TreeExplainer` computes per-prediction feature attributions on the underlying XGBoost estimator (extracted from the calibration wrapper). SHAP values are returned for every prediction, enabling feature-level explanations.

### Adverse Action Reason Codes

20 standardised reason codes (R01 through R20) mapped from SHAP-based feature contributions:

| Code | Feature | Explanation |
|------|---------|-------------|
| R01 | annual_income | Annual income insufficient for requested loan amount |
| R02 | loan_to_income | Loan-to-income ratio exceeds lending criteria |
| R03 | debt_to_income | Existing debt obligations too high relative to income |
| R04 | serviceability_ratio | Insufficient income buffer after monthly commitments |
| R05 | expense_to_income | Monthly expenses too high relative to income |
| R06 | credit_score | Credit score below minimum lending threshold |
| R07 | income_credit_interaction | Combined income and credit profile does not meet criteria |
| R08 | has_bankruptcy | Bankruptcy record on file |
| R09 | employment_length | Insufficient employment tenure |
| R10 | employment_stability | Employment stability does not meet minimum requirements |
| R11 | employment_type_* | Employment type assessed as higher risk |
| R12 | loan_amount | Requested loan amount exceeds assessed borrowing capacity |
| R13 | loan_term_months | Requested loan term outside acceptable range |
| R14 | lvr | Loan-to-value ratio exceeds maximum for this product |
| R15 | lvr_x_dti | Combined leverage and debt ratio exceeds risk tolerance |
| R16 | credit_card_burden | Existing credit card commitments too high |
| R17 | existing_credit_card_limit | Total available credit limits exceed policy |
| R18 | has_cosigner | Application assessed without co-signer support |
| R19 | number_of_dependants | Number of dependants affects assessed living expenses |
| R20 | has_hecs | HECS-HELP debt included in serviceability assessment |

For each denied application, the top 4 most negative SHAP contributors are selected (de-duplicated by reason code). Reapplication guidance is generated with counterfactual explanations and estimated review timelines (3-24 months depending on the adverse factors).

### Counterfactual Explanations

For denied applications, a binary search over the top 3 contributing features finds the minimum change required to flip the decision. Returns actionable statements such as "Increasing credit score from 620 to 715 would change the outcome".

### WOE / IV Scorecard

Weight of Evidence binning and Information Value computation for all numeric features, providing a traditional interpretable scorecard alongside the XGBoost model. A WOE logistic regression scorecard is also built (base score 600, PDO 20) with out-of-sample AUC reported.

### SHAP Explainability: Stability Caveat

This system uses TreeSHAP for generating adverse action reason codes. TreeSHAP
provides exact Shapley values for tree-based models, avoiding the sampling instability
of KernelSHAP. However, documented stability limitations exist:

- **Top and bottom features are stable** in their SHAP rankings across model runs
- **Mid-ranked features show high variability** in importance rankings (arXiv:2508.01851)
- **Class imbalance amplifies instability** — explanations for minority-class predictions
  (defaults) may be less stable than for majority-class (performing)

Mitigation: Adverse action notices report only the top 4 principal reasons, which
fall within the stable ranking zone. The CFPB Circular 2022-03 is method-agnostic —
it requires "specific and accurate reasons" but does not mandate SHAP.

**References:**
- arXiv:2508.01851 — "SHAP Stability in Credit Risk Management" (2025).
- CFPB Circular 2022-03 — consumerfinance.gov/compliance/circulars/circular-2022-03.
- Mougan et al. (2025). "Evaluating stability of model explanations." arXiv:2509.01409.

### Decile Analysis

Predictions are partitioned into 10 deciles by predicted probability. Per-decile actual rates, cumulative rates, and lift values are computed for model discrimination assessment.

## Risk Management

### Population Stability Index (PSI)

- **Overall PSI**: computed on predicted probability distributions (training reference vs. production).
- **Per-feature PSI**: computed for each numeric feature individually.
- **Thresholds**: PSI < 0.10 = stable; 0.10-0.25 = moderate shift (investigate); >= 0.25 = significant shift (retrain).
- **Reference distribution**: percentile summaries and histogram bin counts from the training set, stored in the model bundle.

### Drift Monitoring Limitations

The industry-standard PSI thresholds (0.10 / 0.25) originate from Lewis (1994) and
have **no statistical foundation** — they are arbitrary industry convention that does
not account for sample size or number of categories (Yurdakul, 2020; Siddiqi, 2023).

This system supplements PSI with Kolmogorov-Smirnov testing (p-value based) and
Characteristic Stability Index (CSI) per-feature monitoring to provide statistically
grounded drift detection alongside the heuristic PSI thresholds.

**References:**
- Yurdakul, B. (2020). "Statistical Properties of the Population Stability Index."
  *Journal of Risk Model Validation*, 14(4), 89-100.
- Siddiqi, N. (2023). "The Population Resemblance Statistic." arXiv:2307.11878.
- Gittins & Maydon (2023). "A critical review of PSI." arXiv:2303.01227.

### Per-Application Drift Detection

At inference time, each input feature is checked against the training distribution. Features beyond 3 standard deviations from the training mean generate warnings; beyond 4 standard deviations trigger mandatory human review.

### Adversarial Validation

A logistic regression classifier is trained to distinguish training data from test data (3-fold CV). AUC near 0.50 indicates the distributions are indistinguishable (desirable). AUC > 0.55 triggers a warning; AUC > 0.60 indicates distribution mismatch.

### Concentration Risk (APRA APS 221)

Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) computed by loan purpose, employment type, and state. Thresholds: HHI < 0.15 = well-diversified; 0.15-0.25 = moderate concentration; > 0.25 = high concentration (APRA trigger). Single-segment shares exceeding 40% are also flagged.

### Stress Testing

#### Portfolio-Level (5 scenarios)

| Scenario | Rate increase | Income reduction | Property decline |
|----------|---------------|------------------|------------------|
| Rate shock +2% | +2% | 0% | 0% |
| Rate shock +3% | +3% | 0% | 0% |
| Unemployment rise | 0% | -15% | 0% |
| Property decline 20% | 0% | 0% | -20% |
| Combined adverse | +2% | -15% | -20% |

Portfolio stress tests run across all approved loans, computing at-risk count, at-risk percentage, total exposure, and at-risk exposure percentage. Default is triggered when monthly surplus < 0 or stressed LVR > 1.0.

#### Per-Application (4 scenarios)

Each individual prediction also runs 4 stress scenarios: income -15%, property value -20%, credit score -50, and a combined stress. Results show how the approval probability and decision change under adverse conditions.

### Expected Loss (Basel III / APRA APS 113)

Expected Loss = PD x LGD x EAD, computed per application.

- **PD**: 1 - approval probability (from the model).
- **LGD**: lookup by loan purpose and LVR band, adjusted +/-20% by credit score. Home loans: 0.15 (LVR < 60%) to 0.40 (LVR > 90%). Auto: 0.50. Personal/education: 0.75. Business: 0.80.
- **EAD**: loan amount (simplified).

## Limitations

1. **Trained on synthetic data**, not real lending outcomes. Model performance on real borrower data is unvalidated.
2. **No real credit bureau integration** — bureau features (enquiries, arrears, defaults, BNPL accounts) are simulated, not sourced from Equifax or Illion.
3. **No temporal default patterns** — survival analysis is not implemented. The model makes a single point-in-time prediction and does not model time-to-default.
4. **No behavioural scoring over time** — the model does not incorporate post-origination repayment behaviour.
5. **Geographic granularity at state level only** — postcode-level risk differentiation is not available.
6. **Overfitting gap of approximately 0.07** between training and test AUC. Expected to narrow with larger and more diverse training data.
7. **Static macroeconomic inputs** — macro features (cash rate, unemployment, property growth, consumer confidence) are point-in-time and do not reflect forward-looking scenarios unless explicitly stress-tested.

## Ethical Considerations

- **No protected characteristics used as features.** The model does not include age, gender, ethnicity, marital status, or disability as input features.
- **Fairness metrics monitored** across employment type, applicant type, and geography. Disparate impact ratio must pass the EEOC 80% rule (>= 0.80).
- **Fairness reweighting** during training equalises group representation, preventing the model from systematically disadvantaging under-represented employment types.
- **Monotonic constraints** (33 of 48 numeric features) prevent paradoxical outcomes. A higher credit score cannot produce a worse outcome, all else being equal. A bankruptcy flag cannot improve approval chances.
- **Bias detection pipeline** reviews all generated customer communications (denial emails, next best offers) for discriminatory or inappropriate language.
- **Human-in-the-loop escalation** for low-confidence predictions where |probability - threshold| <= 0.10, and for applications with significant feature drift (z-score > 4.0 from training mean).

## Model Security

- **Path validation**: model files must reside within the configured `ML_MODELS_DIR` and have a `.joblib` extension.
- **Integrity verification**: SHA-256 hash of the model file is checked against the stored hash before loading. Tampered files are rejected.
- **Bounded cache**: at most 3 model bundles are held in memory; older entries are evicted to prevent unbounded memory growth.
- **Pipeline consistency check**: before saving, the trainer validates that categorical columns, feature columns, imputation values, and reference distributions are all present and consistent between the trainer and predictor.
